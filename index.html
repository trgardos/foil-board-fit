Speed & angle of attack

In steady flight total lift always equals your weight, so faster ≠ more lift. What speed changes is the angle of attack — which nudges where the front wing's lift acts.

Geometry above is defined at the reference speed. Going faster lowers the angle of attack and the front wing's centre of pressure drifts aft (up to CoP travel cm); slower pushes it forward. Set CoP travel to 0 for pure statics. Any consistent speed unit works — only the ratio matters.

How to read it & use it for board shopping

The balance point, measured forward of the mast base, is a + f·L — the wing's forward offset plus the small extra shift from the tail download. Placing your combined CG there and solving for your stance centre: r* = ( W·(a + f·L) − W_board·b ) / W_you, where b is the board CG's distance ahead of the mast. That r* is nearly a constant of your foil — the board term is tiny.

Does speed matter? For fore–aft balance, barely. In steady flight the foil always makes lift equal to your weight — going faster just lowers the angle of attack, it doesn't add lift, so the force arrows don't grow. The one real trim effect is that a lower angle of attack lets the front wing's centre of pressure drift aft, so the effective a shrinks a little at speed and your balanced stance creeps back. The speed card models exactly that, as an adjustable CoP travel; set it to zero for pure statics.

The track question. Your feet sit on the footpad (fixed on the board). The foil slides in the track, which moves the mast base relative to that pad. So the real test is: with the mast somewhere in the track, does your balanced stance r* land on the pad? The tool computes the mast position that would centre your stance on the pad — mast = padCentre − r* — and flags whether that falls inside the track range. If it doesn't, that board can't give you your trim without standing off the pad, no matter the track length.

Slide the mast and the board pitches: your feet stay put on the pad while the lift point moves under them, so the CG drifts off the lift line until you re-place your feet (drag yourself) or move the mast back. Cap on the drawn tilt is 13° for legibility — the honest number is the CG-offset readout.
